## LinkedIn

When Dutch students contact university student-life officers about roommate tension, two themes dominate the intake notes: cleanliness and noise.

That is not surprising. What is striking is how often students admit they never raised the issue directly, assuming a housemate should already know something is wrong.

New editorial on Domu Match connects that pattern to wider wellbeing signals:

- Gezondheidsmonitor Jongvolwassenen 2024 (GGD / RIVM): only about half of 16–25-year-olds report good mental health
- Resto VanHarte / Panel Inzicht (2025): 48% of Dutch young adults aged 18–34 felt lonely in the preceding three months

The article outlines a practical de-escalation sequence drawn from Dutch university guidance: behaviour-focused feedback, written house norms, scheduled repair conversations, and three concrete changes per party.

Conflict in shared living is normal. Escalation is optional.

## Instagram

That dish left in the sink for "just one night"? By week three it feels like a message about your boundaries.

Most roommate blow-ups in Dutch student houses start small: noise after midnight, a partner who "just stays over," cleaning standards nobody wrote down.

University student-life officers say cleanliness and noise are the top reasons students ask for help. And many admit they never said anything directly first.

48% of Dutch young adults (18–34) reported feeling lonely in the past three months (Resto VanHarte, 2025). A tense home won't cause that alone, but it can make everything heavier.

Five de-escalation moves that actually work:

1. Name the behaviour, not the person
2. Write expectations down before you need them
3. Schedule a repair talk (don't ambush)
4. Agree on three measurable changes each
5. Know when to involve support beyond the kitchen table

What's the smallest conflict in your student house that turned into something bigger because nobody spoke up early?
